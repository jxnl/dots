# Writing Check

Pressure-test the post before handing it back. Judge what a new reader can see, not what the speaker meant to say.

## Audience and message

- Can the reader tell who the post is for and repeat its main message in one sentence?
- Does it name a pain they want to stop or a result they want?
- Does it explain any term or person a new reader would not know?

## Attention

- Does the title name a reader, result, pain, proof, or useful tension?
- Do the first lines tell the reader why they should stop?
- Can the title, hook, or opening lose words without losing meaning?
- Does the opening promise what the body delivers?

## Interest and proof

- Does each section add a claim, example, action, or result?
- Is there a reason to read the next paragraph?
- Does each important claim have an example, source, or clear limit?
- Are two sections making the same point? Keep the one with the better example.
- Could a skeptic tell what actually happened?

## Desire and action

- What can the reader understand, feel, or do by the end?
- How much time or work does the first useful step take?
- Is there one clear action: try, reply, share, follow, click, or change a habit?
- Does the reader know what they get for taking it?

## Voice and integrity

- Which lines sound like the speaker? Which sound like a model or press release?
- Remove vague praise and filler such as `powerful`, `seamless`, `important`, and `innovative`. Use an example, number, action, or limit instead.
- State the claim directly. Cut repeated `not X, but Y` frames.
- Keep facts, opinion, and guesses separate.
- Never invent a result, quote, anecdote, or reaction to make the post stronger.

If a gap changes the post, ask one question. Recommend the answer supported by the transcript.
