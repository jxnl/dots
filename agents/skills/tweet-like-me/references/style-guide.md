# Style Guide

Source: latest 400 `@jxnlco` tweets fetched on 2026-05-24. The corpus was roughly half original posts and half replies, with many short reactions, Codex/product posts, and practical asks.

## Core Voice

- Casual, direct, present-tense, and product-native.
- Sounds like a builder watching the thing happen in real time, not a marketer announcing a feature.
- Mixes practical usefulness with low-friction internet speech.
- Often uses a tiny joke or blunt aside, then lands on a concrete ask or observation.
- Comfortable with imperfect grammar, lowercase starts, short fragments, and abrupt reactions.
- Lets specificity carry credibility: product names, workflows, commands, UI surfaces, models, teams, and user situations.

## Corpus Fingerprint

- Length: median around 56 characters, mean around 75, with occasional 2-4 paragraph posts for tips or feedback asks.
- Shape: most posts are one-liners; longer posts use blank lines between short thoughts.
- Replies: about half the corpus; many are one sentence or less.
- Links: common, often with only a short reaction.
- Punctuation: sparse. Questions appear when collecting feedback. Exclamation points are rare.
- Common language: `codex`, `app`, `use`, `new`, `openai`, `chatgpt`, `computer`, `team`, `ask`, `goal`, `try`, `dm me`, `let me know`, `what do you need from us`.

## Do

- Start close to the thought. Skip throat-clearing.
- Use concrete verbs: `ask`, `try`, `use`, `run`, `make`, `ship`, `open`, `fix`.
- Use plain asks: `what do you need from us`, `let me know`, `dm me`, `hit me with all of your frustrations`.
- Let product details be a little raw: `cloudflare on safari`, `create table commands`, `CMD+CMD`, `/goal`, `skills installer`.
- Allow casual fragments: `big if true`, `noted`, `thnx`, `woah`, `pardon`, `try opus`.
- Use line breaks for emphasis in higher-stakes posts.
- Keep the feeling slightly underproduced. A little messy is more authentic than perfectly copywritten.

## Avoid

- Do not sound like SaaS marketing: no polished launch-copy cadence, no "unlock your potential", no generic "game changer".
- Do not over-explain. Most drafts should be shorter than the average assistant instinct.
- Do not add hashtags unless the user explicitly asks.
- Do not add emoji unless the source context already uses one or the user asks.
- Do not invent private company details, internal plans, personal experiences, or promises.
- Do not make every tweet funny. Many are simply useful, direct, or observational.
- Do not sand away all rough edges; keep some lowercase, fragments, or abruptness when it fits.

## Common Moves

- **Useful discovery:** "If you're using X, you might not know about Y..."
- **Feedback ask:** "If you're using X today, what feels missing?"
- **Live product observation:** "Watching codex do X from Y."
- **Tiny reaction:** "Wow", "Lmfao", ":O", "thnx", plus link.
- **Fast support:** "dm me", "what do you need from us", "let me know what you learn".
- **Operator take:** "You gotta make things cause it's important for the audience to see them."
- **Self-deprecating practicality:** admits roughness, dogfooding, or a hacky workflow without apologizing too much.

## Rewrite Checklist

- Is it concrete enough that a real user can try something?
- Could it be 30% shorter?
- Does it sound like a person mid-conversation?
- Is there one clear job: react, ask, teach, invite, or state a take?
- Did it avoid inventing facts Jason did not provide?
